## Motivation
* Autonomous drone swarms increasingly rely on complex AI-driven sensor fusion and visual tracking algorithms for coordination and navigation.
* Current defense mechanisms focus heavily on cyber-intrusions (e.g., GPS spoofing or RF jamming), ignoring vulnerabilities in the physical environment perception layer.
* Adversaries can deploy stealthy, real-world visual perturbations that bypass digital alarms, leading to drone capture or catastrophic swarm-level Byzantine failures.
* The "distance-pulling" attack vector exposes how drones programmed for dynamic active tracking can be lured into compromised physical positions.

## Contribution
* Analyzes the "FlyTrap" distance-pulling framework, detailing how specifically patterned physical objects deceive the neural network's depth estimation.
* Demonstrates how compromising a single node's visual perception effectively disrupts the distributed coordination logic of the entire swarm.
* Highlights severe security vulnerabilities in commercial and military multi-sensor fusion algorithms operating in unconstrained physical environments.
* Proposes robust algorithmic defenses incorporating adversarial anomaly detection and cross-sensor cross-validation to reject stealthy physical spoofs.
